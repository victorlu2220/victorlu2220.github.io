Artificial Bone with a Monitored Bioreactor for Testing


Abstract:

      Unicameral Bone Cysts(UBCs) are found primarily in children and cause severe pain if left untreated. During treatment, surgeons extract fluid from the cysts and fill the void with bone-graft biomaterial. This surgical treatment can be expensive and requires a longer recovery period. In order to minimize cost and recovery time, a minimally invasive treatment procedure is ideal. The goal of this project is to create a bone-graft biomaterial that is easily injected into the USB that can still mimic the mechanical properties of natural bone. To test this biomaterial, a bioreactor will also be fabricated to test the samples in an environment that mimics the in vivo conditions in the body. The final injectable biomaterial will help make UBS treatment more accessible to patients by providing a minimally invasive treatment that lowers cost and recovery time.

Software:

      An Arduino code has been written to create a temperature-sensitive circuit. Voltage is measured from the thermistor, then resistance was calculated and was converted to temperature based on the resistance-temperature relationship calculated from the datasheet of the thermistor. The LED of the circuit is turned on when the temperature reaches 35.5 degrees Celcius; it then experiences a buffer temerature range to prevent being turned on and off frequently at the threshold temperature. The LED won't be turned off till the temperature reaches 36.5 degrees Celcius. The code is written in a way that a boolean controls the state of LED, and it will only change if the temperature reaches 35.5 and then 36.5(the order does not matter) degree consecutively.

Bone Samples:

      Six simple bone grafts were fabricated. Their physical properties should be similar to that of native bones. For this experiment, it was decided that, for the sake of being both time and cost efficiency, the bone samples were made by mixing calcium sulphate and deionized water. Half of the samples were then placed within the bioreactor for in-vitro testing, the rest were used as a control group.

Testing:

      The system was tested for 2 weeks. After two weeks, experimental group was taken out. An MTS machine was used in order to measure the stress-strain relationship of the bone grafts(both control and experimental groups), so that Young's modulus could be calculated when doing the data analysis.

Results and Data Analysis:

      pH of the fluid remain unchanged based on the color of phenol-red. MTS machine recorded the stress and strain value when a compression test was being operated. The following data analysis are done using MatLab, data was imported from both EEPROM and MTS machine.



Figure XIV: Temperature Display in EEPROM. Data was recorded every 15 seconds for 2 weeks.





Figure XV: Temperature Display Boxplot.





Figure XVI: Stress-Strain Curve. MTS machine recorded both force(F) and change in length(delt(L)). The cross sectional area(A) of each sample was measured as well as their original length(L) previous to experiment. Stress and strain was then calculated respectively by F/A, delt(L)/L. Only the roughly linear portion of the graph was taken for the resulting Young's modulus to be accurate.





Figure XVII: Boxplot for Young's Modulus. Young's modulus describes how much a material deforms under pressure. The greater it is, the less deformation there will be under the same stress.



Some Statistics:

  • Mean Young's Modulus:   experimental group is 303.79 MPa, control group is 184.65 MPa

  • Student t-Test:   p-value is .32, suggesting there's not sufficient statistical evidence to reject null hypothesis.

  • Coefficient of Variance:   experimental group has a CV of .42, control group has a CV of .36

    •

    Conclusion:

        Native bones have Young's modulus range from 10 to 3000 MPa. The average Young's modulus of the bone grafts created fall well between this range. P-value of the Student t-test suggests that there is not enough information to conclude that there is a statistically significant difference between 2 groups. This is a good sign because a short, 2-week testing shouldn't have led to a significant change in physical properties. However, a good coefficient of variance(CV) is usually below .3, the CV of both groups are therefore not ideal. This yields the concern of inconsistency across samples.

    Future Considerations:

  • Code:
          The code currently records data relatively rapid(15 seconds) with respect to a 2-week testing period. EEPROM only has 1024 starage places. Therefore, the resulting data set only showcases a snapshot of the variation in temperature and it does not showcase a macroscopic version of how the temperature varies. A longer interval could be considered.

  • Bioreactor:
          In-vivo condition has a mobile fluid system. Thus, a spinning component could be integrated within the bioreactor to stir the fluid and mimic such a condition, which will promote the precision of the outcome of the experiment.

  • Bone Graft Samples:
          Current bone graft samples are simply mechanically comparable to native bones. Its chemical properties are yet to be tested. Moreover, they should be advanced to a level that can enable angiogenesis and the growth of osteoblast. The degradation rate should also be measured so that it could match with that of the native bones. Chemicals such as hydroxypropyl methyl cellulose, polyethylene glycol and methyl cellulose could be integrated to bone grafts to improve their compressive strength and injectabiity.
